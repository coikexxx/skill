# Risk Signals

Use these signals to detect likely GEO manipulation or AI-content poisoning.

## High-signal red flags

- Open-ended question gets a very specific brand-first answer.
- Multiple sources repeat the same selling points with only light wording changes.
- Pages contain polished claims but no original data, method, or primary citation.
- Strong superlatives appear: “best”, “number one”, “industry-leading”, “top choice”.
- Claimed strengths are detailed, while weaknesses or tradeoffs are missing.
- A new or obscure product suddenly has abundant positive writeups.
- Many “comparison”, “review”, or “ranking” pages point in the same direction without showing independent testing.
- Sources appear independent but are really syndications, rewrites, or self-media reposts.

## Medium-signal red flags

- Author identity is unclear or generic.
- Site covers many unrelated domains with templated article structure.
- Page has strong buyer intent language but weak factual grounding.
- Evidence depends on screenshots, testimonials, or uncited feature tables.
- AI answers keep circling back to one brand under many prompt variations.

## Extra caution domains

Raise suspicion faster in:

- software/tool/vendor recommendations
- medical, legal, finance, and security advice
- education/training provider comparisons
- repair, local services, and high-trust categories
- products where affiliate economics are strong

## What not to over-flag

Do not label content as manipulated merely because:

- it is well structured
- the brand is genuinely prominent
- multiple strong sources agree
- the answer includes one specific product after explicit filtering criteria

The issue is not specificity by itself; the issue is **specificity without strong evidence**.
